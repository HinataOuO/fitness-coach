## 1. Why plyometrics for bodyweight lower body training

Bodyweight lower body training has a known limitation: once you can do pistol squats and nordic curls, further overloading is difficult without adding external weight. Plyometric training fills this gap by targeting **explosive power and neuromuscular reactivity** — qualities that pure strength exercises don't develop adequately.

**What plyometrics adds that static strength exercises don't:**
- Trains the stretch-shortening cycle (SSC) — the elastic energy mechanism
- Develops Rate of Force Development (RFD) — how fast force is produced
- Improves neuromuscular coordination and reactive strength
- Increases leg stiffness (beneficial for performance)
- Applicable to calisthenics power movements (explosive pull-ups, muscle-up, bar muscle-up)
- Zero equipment needed for most progressions

## 2. Stretch-Shortening Cycle (SSC)

The SSC is the physiological basis of plyometric training. (NSCA, Essentials of S&C)

**Mechanism:**
1. **Eccentric phase** — muscle lengthens rapidly under load (e.g., landing in a squat before jumping)
2. **Amortization phase** — brief isometric transition (the shorter this is, the more elastic energy is retained)
3. **Concentric phase** — explosive muscle shortening, amplified by stored elastic energy + stretch reflex

**Why it matters:** a countermovement jump (CMJ) is 15–20% higher than a static squat jump with the same strength level, purely due to SSC utilization.

**Two types of SSC:**
| Type | Contact time | Examples |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Slow SSC** | >250ms | CMJ, broad jump, squat jump |
| **Fast SSC** | <250ms | Depth jump, ankle hops, bounding |

Both types are trainable and produce different adaptations. A complete program includes both.

## 3. Evidence on plyometric effectiveness

**Meta-analysis — Scientific Reports (Nature), 2025 — 70 RCTs, 1703 trained adults:**
Plyometric training significantly improves lower limb strength, power, agility, and jump performance in trained adults.

**Meta-analysis — Sports Medicine Open, 2023 (Ramirez-Campillo et al.):**
- Minimum effective dose: **4 weeks, 8 sessions, ~92 jumps/week**
- Improves: vertical jump, horizontal jump, sprint speed, reactive strength index
- Similar benefits in males and females

**Meta-analysis — PMC, 2023 — Reactive Strength Index:**
PJT (Plyometric Jump Training) significantly improves RSI — a key marker of SSC efficiency and athletic performance — across all age groups.

**NSCA Strength & Conditioning Journal (2016):**
- Plyometric training improves sprint times (5–40m), maximal muscle power, and injury prevention markers
- Recommended: 2 sessions/week for most training contexts
- Performance improvements documented in programs under 10 weeks

## 4. Exercise classification by intensity

### Low intensity (beginners, warm-up, rehabilitation)
- Ankle hops in place
- Jump rope (slow)
- Skipping
- Lateral shuffles
- Two-foot small box jumps (low height)
- Jumping jacks
- Mini hurdle hops

### Medium intensity (intermediate)
- Squat jump (no countermovement)
- Countermovement jump (CMJ)
- Broad jump (horizontal)
- Lateral bounds
- Box jump (moderate height)
- Tuck jump
- Split squat jump

### High intensity (advanced)
- Depth jump (step off box, land and immediately jump)
- Bounding (single-leg horizontal)
- Single-leg box jump
- Single-leg broad jump
- Hurdle jumps (high)
- Reactive drop jump

**Key principle (NSCA):** when intensity increases, volume must decrease. Never combine high intensity + high volume.

## 5. Progressions: beginner to advanced

### Phase 1 — Foundation (weeks 1–4)
**Goal:** learn landing mechanics, develop basic SSC awareness

Prerequisites: can hold single-leg stance for 30 seconds, basic squat form

```
Session structure (2×/week):
- Ankle hops in place:     3 × 10
- Squat jump:              3 × 6
- Broad jump:              3 × 5
- Lateral bound:           3 × 5/side
Total contacts: ~80–100/session
Recovery: 2–3 min between sets
```

**Landing technique focus:**
- Land softly, absorb shock through ankle → knee → hip
- Knees tracking over toes (no valgus collapse)
- Chest up, slight forward lean
- Avoid stiff-legged landing

### Phase 2 — Development (weeks 5–8)
**Goal:** increase SSC efficiency, add reactive elements

```
Session structure (2×/week):
- CMJ (countermovement jump):    4 × 5
- Box jump (moderate height):    3 × 5
- Tuck jump:                     3 × 6
- Split squat jump:              3 × 5/side
- Lateral bound continuous:      3 × 6/side
Total contacts: ~100–120/session
```

### Phase 3 — Power (weeks 9–12)
**Goal:** maximum power expression, fast SSC training

```
Session structure (2×/week):
- Depth jump (low box):          4 × 4  (full rest 2–3 min)
- Single-leg broad jump:         3 × 4/side
- Reactive continuous jumps:     3 × 8
- Bounding:                      3 × 10 steps
Total contacts: ~80–100/session (lower because intensity is higher)
```

### Phase 4 — Maintenance / integration (ongoing)
Once power goals are reached, maintain with 1 session/week at moderate intensity.

## 6. NSCA volume and frequency guidelines

**Volume** is measured in **ground contacts** (foot touches per session):

| Level | Contacts/session | Sessions/week |
|-------|-----------------|---------------|
| Beginner | 80–100 | 2 |
| Intermediate | 100–150 | 2 |
| Advanced | 120–200 | 2–3 |

**Key rule:** intensity and volume are inversely proportional. High-intensity sessions (depth jumps, bounding) → lower contact count.

**Frequency:**
- 2 sessions/week is optimal for most non-athletes (NSCA, S&C Journal 2016)
- Minimum 48h rest between plyometric sessions
- Never 2 consecutive days of plyometric training

**Program length:**
- Minimum effective: 4 weeks
- Optimal for significant adaptations: 6–10 weeks
- Vertical jump improvements visible as early as 4 weeks

## 7. Integration with strength and calisthenics

### Weekly template — bodyweight lower body focused

```
Monday:    Strength lower body (pistol squat progressions + nordic curl)
Tuesday:   Upper body calisthenics
Wednesday: REST or mobility
Thursday:  Plyometrics (2×/week session A)
Friday:    Upper body strength/skill
Saturday:  Plyometrics (2×/week session B) OR active recovery
Sunday:    REST
```

### Key integration rules

1. **Plyometrics and heavy strength same day:** possible for advanced athletes (complex training), NOT recommended for beginners. If done together, strength first → plyometrics second.

2. **Plyometrics and running same day:** avoid on the same day if both are high intensity. Low-intensity running (Z2) after plyometrics is acceptable.

3. **Interference with upper body calisthenics:** minimal — plyometrics primarily tax the lower body and CNS. Schedule skill work (planche, front lever) on days separate from leg-heavy plyometric sessions.

4. **Complex training (advanced):** pairing a heavy compound movement with a plyometric exercise exploits post-activation potentiation (PAP):
   - Squat heavy 3–5 reps → rest 3–4 min → CMJ × 5
   - Nordic curl × 4 → rest 3 min → broad jump × 5

### Calisthenics power transfer
Plyometric training directly improves:
- **Muscle-up explosive pull:** fast SSC of lats and biceps (applies to bar muscle-up)
- **Explosive push-ups / clapping push-ups:** upper body plyometrics complement lower body
- **Dynamic freestyle calisthenics:** all explosive movements benefit

## 8. Testing and tracking jump performance

### Countermovement Jump (CMJ) — primary test
**How:** stand upright, dip quickly and jump as high as possible, land and measure.
**Equipment:** measuring tape on wall (mark highest reach standing vs jumping) or phone app (My Jump 2, etc.)
**Test frequency:** every 4 weeks
**Track:** cm of jump height improvement

### Standing Broad Jump — horizontal power
**How:** from standing, jump forward as far as possible with two feet, measure distance from takeoff line to landing point.
**Track:** distance in cm

### Reactive Strength Index (RSI) — fast SSC quality
RSI = jump height / ground contact time
Requires a contact mat or app. More advanced metric — use if available.

### Practical tracking in log
Add to weekly log when test week:
```
📏 Test W[N]:
CMJ: ___ cm (vs W[N-4]: ___ cm)
Broad jump: ___ cm
Notes: ___
```

## 9. Safety and prerequisites

### Before starting plyometrics

**NSCA prerequisite (official guideline):** back squat ≥ 1.5× bodyweight before high-intensity plyometrics (depth jumps, bounding).

**Practical note:** this threshold applies to high-intensity exercises. Low-to-medium intensity plyometrics (jumps, CMJ, broad jump) can start earlier if basic squat mechanics are solid.

**Minimum prerequisites for beginner program:**
- Single-leg stance for 30 sec without falling
- Single-leg half squat for 30 sec
- Proper squat form (no knee valgus, full ROM)
- No acute lower limb injuries

### Landing mechanics — critical
Poor landing = most common plyometric injury cause.

**Correct landing:**
- Soft, progressive absorption (ankle → knee → hip)
- Knees over toes, no valgus
- Hip hinge, slight forward lean
- Quiet landing (loud = too stiff)

**Never progress intensity until landing mechanics are consistently correct.**

### Surfaces
- Grass, rubber mat, wooden gym floor: optimal
- Hard concrete: acceptable for experienced athletes, not beginners
- Soft foam/thick mat: reduces SSC benefits (too much absorption)

### Body weight consideration
NSCA recommends athletes over 100kg avoid depth jumps from heights above 45cm due to increased joint stress.
